1. Executive Summary

Systemic risks of compliance-by-design

This report evaluates the "compliance-by-design" stablecoin architecture proposed by Duffie, Olowookere, and Veneris (DOV). The analysis concludes that despite innovative cryptographic components, the model creates systemic data honeypots, increases the risk of financial surveillance, and is structurally incompatible with global medium-of-exchange needs due to jurisdictional fragmentation. This design also poses significant human-rights risks, enabling authoritarian misuse through centralized credential revocation and programmable financial controls.

As an alternative, the report recommends a layered model combining (1) a privacy-preserving, neutral base-layer protocol such as Zcash, and (2) decentralized, open identity standards such as Coin Center's John Hancock Project. This combination offers privacy-by-default, selective disclosure, reduced centralization of personal data, and greater resilience against political abuse.

2. Introduction

Understanding the DOV proposal

The IMF Finance & Development article "The Stablecoin Balancing Act" outlines a "compliance-by-design" architecture for stablecoin payment systems. This approach seeks to reconcile user privacy with the requirements of Anti-Money Laundering (AML), Countering the Financing of Terrorism (CFT), and sanctions compliance by moving enforcement from ex-post, off-chain monitoring to real-time, on-chain enforcement.

The core elements of the DOV model include:

  • KYC Perimeter: Users obtain verified identity from a licensed "credential issuer," which places a hashed cryptographic commitment of the credential on the stablecoin ledger.
  • zkKYC and Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs): Transactions are verified using ZKPs, proving the user holds a valid credential and meets KYC criteria without revealing their identity on-chain.
  • On-chain Compliance Engine: Embedded smart contracts monitor transactions for red flags and automatically generate Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs), with the ability to delay or block transfers.
  • Layered Transparency: Identities are unmasked only following a legal process (warrant, subpoena) triggered by a SAR.
  • Governance: The system assumes a network of licensed credential issuers and smart-contract operators, with common standards and interoperability.

This report challenges the underlying assumptions of the compliance-by-design model, arguing that its concentration of power and data creates significant systemic risks that outweigh its purported compliance benefits.

3. Critique of the Compliance-by-Design Stablecoin Model

Why embedding compliance into consensus is risky

3.1

Structural Data Honeypots and Re-Identification Risk

Even with the use of ZKPs and hashed credentials, the DOV model mandates the creation of large, highly attractive stores of sensitive data, maximizing the "blast radius" of any breach:

  • Credential Issuer Databases: Issuers must maintain "databases of validated user credentials" containing real-world identity documents linked to on-chain addresses.
  • Linkability through Compromise: A breach of a major credential issuer would allow an attacker to link off-chain identities to on-chain hashes and the aggregated financial histories they represent.
  • Centralized SAR Logic: Compliance smart contracts and risk models themselves represent critical intelligence, detailing law enforcement thresholds and targeting patterns.
  • De-anonymization Risk: Transaction metadata combined with external data often proves sufficient for de-anonymization, regardless of the strength of on-chain hashing or ZKPs.

The architecture fundamentally increases systemic risk by centralizing identity, compliance logic, and transaction-level risk scoring in a small number of supervised intermediaries.

3.2

Fragmented Global Governance and Medium-of-Exchange Limitations

The DOV model's reliance on embedding national legal standards into consensus logic creates structural problems for its function as a global medium of exchange:

  • Divergent Risk Lists: Major jurisdictions will not align on risk lists or sanctions policies. A single global ledger cannot enforce incompatible rule sets without severe fragmentation.
  • Extraterritorial Control: If a dominant jurisdiction controls the smart-contract logic, its sanctions lists become de facto global infrastructure, undermining other nations' sovereignty.
  • Jurisdictional Arbitrage: Users and issuers will gravitate toward permissive regimes, driving the fragmentation the model seeks to avoid.

A system whose ability to send funds depends on conflicting national rule sets embedded in consensus logic is destined to fragment along geopolitical lines.

3.3

Human Rights and Authoritarian Misuse

  • Credential Revocation as Deplatforming: Administrative revocation can exclude opponents from economic life.
  • Low Due-Process Thresholds: Weak legal safeguards can enable rapid unmasking without independent review.
  • Mission Creep and Censorship: SAR criteria can be expanded to political categories, automating censorship.

3.4

Technical and Computational Constraints

  • Computational Overhead: Per-transaction zkKYC proofs and compliance checks add latency and cost.
  • Smart-Contract Complexity: Constantly updating regulatory logic risks bugs and network halts.
  • Denial-of-Service Risk: Attackers could stress compliance logic to destabilize the system.
  • Specialized Cryptography Dependence: Complex ZK systems increase the odds of subtle, high-impact bugs.

3.5

Governance, Concentration, and Capture

  • Regulatory Capture: Dominant financial institutions may shape rules in ways that erode privacy and competition.
  • Single Points of Failure: Compromise of a major issuer or SAR logic operator can affect large portions of the network.
  • Opaque Rule-Making: Critical interpretations of "suspicious activity" could be determined with limited public oversight.

3.6

Limited Incremental AML Benefit

Existing KYC/AML regimes are empirically ineffective, intercepting an estimated less than 1 percent of illicit funds globally. The DOV model intensifies this paradigm - centralized identity collection and universal pre-screening - rather than questioning its efficacy. Given the high costs in privacy, resilience, and human rights, the policy justification for embedding this ineffective compliance model into the base layer is weak.

4. Coin Center's John Hancock Project: A Different Identity and Compliance Model

Open, interoperable, and privacy-preserving credentials

4.1

Core Features of the John Hancock Project

  • Open, Interoperable Standards: Drafts decentralized, privacy-preserving credential standards any vendor or institution can implement.
  • Decentralized, User-Held Identity: Verifiable credentials are held by users and shared selectively, eliminating centralized identity databases.
  • Self-Sovereign Attestations: Entities issue cryptographic attestations and "self-sovereign risk scores" provable in zero knowledge.
  • Constructive Government Role: Government recognizes open standards as acceptable compliance methods rather than operating core infrastructure.

4.2

How JHP Addresses Core Concerns

Honeypots and Data Centralization

Credentials are user-held and portable, minimizing central databases. ZK proofs and selective disclosure lower the value of any single breach.

Global Compatibility

Standards are payment-rail agnostic, letting jurisdictions apply their own screening without forcing a global blacklist into consensus logic.

Authoritarian Misuse

Decentralized, user-controlled attestations remove the central switch that would allow system-wide exclusion of dissidents.

5. Zcash as a Base-Layer Alternative: Privacy-First Money with Optional Compliance

Decoupling compliance from consensus

5.1

Zcash Architecture in Brief

Zcash utilizes zk-SNARKs to support shielded transactions, where amounts and counterparties are encrypted on-chain.

Selective Disclosure via Viewing Keys: Shielded addresses have an associated viewing key that allows read-only access to transaction history without spending authority. Users can selectively share these keys with auditors, regulators, or tax authorities.

5.2

Advantages over Compliance-by-Design Stablecoins

  • Data Minimization: No requirement to register identity or maintain hashed KYC credentials on-chain. The ledger reveals minimal information.
  • Neutral Base Layer: Consensus rules enforce only validity. AML/CFT is implemented at the edges, keeping the base layer politically neutral.
  • Targeted Compliance: Regulated entities can require address-specific viewing keys, providing regulatory visibility without broadcasting data to the world.
  • Resilience: An authoritarian government cannot repurpose the base protocol as a centralized control system because there is no native KYC perimeter or SAR engine to seize.

5.3

Integration of John Hancock-Style Identity with Zcash

The optimal solution is a layered architecture:

  • Base Layer (Zcash): Provides censorship-resistant, privacy-preserving settlement.
  • Identity/Compliance Layer (JHP-style credentials): Provides portable, privacy-preserving credentials and attestations for regulated entities to use for onboarding and targeted reporting.

This combination keeps the rail neutral and private, while allowing sophisticated, privacy-preserving compliance where law or business needs require it.

5.4

Monetary Design and Medium-of-Exchange Function

While ZEC is a floating asset, stable value can be achieved by deploying Zcash-backed or Zcash-settled stablecoins. Issuers can utilize Zcash's shielded layer for privacy and view keys for compliance, while JHP-style credentials govern issuance and redemption, leaving secondary transfers private. The key difference from DOV is that compliance is decoupled from the base-layer transaction.

5.5

Scalability and Performance

Zcash has made steady progress improving shielded performance, most notably through the transition to Halo 2, which removed the need for a trusted setup and reduced proof sizes and verification overhead. These upgrades let the shielded pool operate more efficiently as usage grows.

A major forward-looking improvement is Project Tachyon, an Electric Coin Company initiative to redesign Zcash network architecture for higher throughput, better fee economics, and modernized consensus operations. Tachyon proposes a modular, performance-oriented architecture that can:

  • Increase transaction throughput by redesigning validation and mempool pathways.
  • Improve fee efficiency by reducing competition for block space and enabling more predictable economics.
  • Support future enhancements such as light-client scalability, improved wallet performance, and expanded shielded functionality.
  • Lay groundwork for optional programmability and interoperability without compromising Zcash privacy guarantees.

Tachyon keeps the base layer lightweight, private, and high-performance while enabling richer functionality through layered systems. This avoids the DOV requirement to embed compliance logic in consensus, keeping scalability improvements independent of regulatory complexity.

As Tachyon and related improvements roll out, Zcash is positioned to support:

  • Higher-volume retail and commercial payments
  • Large-scale shielded stablecoin settlement
  • Selective-disclosure business workflows, especially when combined with JHP-style credentials

These changes strengthen Zcash as a privacy-preserving settlement layer that can scale with demand while remaining neutral, resilient, and globally interoperable.

7. Conclusion and Recommendation

A layered path forward

The Duffie-Olowookere-Veneris model, while technically sophisticated, is fundamentally incompatible with the requirements of a resilient, global, and rights-respecting medium of exchange. It centralizes data, embeds politically contestable rules into core infrastructure, and creates a highly governable system prone to mission creep and authoritarian abuse.

The recommended alternative - combining a privacy-preserving, neutral base layer like Zcash with an open, decentralized identity and compliance framework like Coin Center's John Hancock Project - offers a superior path:

  • Privacy by Default: Transactions are private, with selective disclosure via viewing keys and ZK attestations.
  • Compliance Decoupled: Compliance is targeted and risk-based at the institutional edge, not universal at the protocol core.
  • Resilience: The infrastructure is harder to repurpose for authoritarian control because the base layer lacks built-in governance levers.
  • Global Compatibility: Jurisdictions can enforce their own rules at the edge without attempting to control the underlying rail.

This layered approach aligns better with data-minimization principles, civil-liberties norms, and the need for neutral, resilient global payment infrastructure.
